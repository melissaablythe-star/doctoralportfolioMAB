Introduction

Where I Began and Where I Am

Advanced academic programming often looks equitable on paper. Enrollment increases, course offerings expand, and districts announce rising participation rates. Access alone, though, does not produce intellectual equity. In my work supporting Advanced Placement instruction across Fort Worth ISD, I repeatedly observed differences in how rigor was enacted across campuses. In one Title I classroom, students were completing structured note guides and preparing for multiple-choice practice. In a higher-SES campus running the same course, students were engaged in peer critique, metacognitive reflection journals, and iterative revision of analytical writing. Both classrooms were on pace. Both were aligned to standards. The cognitive work required of students was not the same.

I came into this program convinced I knew what the problem was and ready to fix it. The program had other ideas. What followed was two years of coursework, research, collaboration, and reflection that changed not just what I know but how I think.

"I entered this program focused on solving problems. I leave it focused on studying them, which turns out to be a more powerful way to produce lasting change."

This portfolio documents that development. The sections below address each element of the Marshall University Doctoral Portfolio Rubric: Collaboration, Depth of Understanding, Reflection, Scholarship, Communication, and Research. Each section includes the artifacts that support it.

Collaboration

Faculty Collaboration

Working with Dr. Childress on my conceptual framework and NSSA conference presentation required a level of precision I had not previously applied to my thinking. When I brought an early draft for review, the question came back: where is the mechanism? I had assembled theories I valued. What I had not done was show how one thing actually produces another. That challenge forced substantive revision and elevated the work from observation to argument. Coursework with Dr. Yuan produced my Positionality Reflection and Conceptual Framework, both of which required defending theoretical choices rather than simply reporting them. Coursework with Dr. Sochor produced three annotated bibliographies examining AP equity, dual enrollment, gifted education, and the experiences of underrepresented students in advanced academic settings, building the literature foundation that now underpins my dissertation thinking.

Peer Collaboration

The SITE conference panel brought together five doctoral students from different professional contexts to build a cohesive argument about AI and teacher preparation. Our perspectives did not always align at the start, and getting to a coherent argument required real negotiation. Prior to the SITE presentation, I also collaborated with faculty and peers in the development of graduate-level AI course modules, including work on Natural Language Processing and student use of AI tools in learning. This collaborative course development work required synthesizing emerging research on AI and cognition with practical instructional design, and it deepened my understanding of how AI tools can support or undermine metacognitive development depending on how they are positioned within instruction. Across multiple courses, sustained peer dialogue on AI, metacognition, and instructional equity sharpened my thinking in ways that individual study could not replicate. Within coursework, peer critique during pilot study development helped me catch leading questions in my interview protocol before I used them in the field. A colleague pointed out that my framing implied deficit in students rather than in instructional design. That observation reshaped how I write about equity in research contexts, a change that carried forward into the dissertation proposal and the NSSA presentation.

Practitioner Partnership

My action research at Dunbar High School was not a study I conducted on a teacher. It was a project I built with one. Co-planning lessons, modeling strategies, and analyzing student work together over three weeks showed what instructional change actually looks like from the inside. By the final week, the teacher described catching herself mid-lesson and changing a question because she heard the deficit framing in her own voice. That is the kind of shift that professional development rarely produces.

Rubric Element 02

Depth of Understanding

The most significant intellectual shift of this program was conceptual. I stopped viewing instructional improvement as a logistics problem and started understanding it as a philosophical one. Curriculum theory is where that shift began, and my CI 702 presentation, annotated bibliographies, technology paper, and conceptual framework document how it developed across the program.

Curriculum Theory

Eisner's (2002) work reframed curriculum as an intellectual and aesthetic encounter rather than content to be delivered. That framing forced me to ask not just whether students completed tasks but what kind of thinking those tasks required. Freire's (1970) pedagogy of critical consciousness raised a harder question: whose knowledge gets centered, and who decides what rigor looks like? In AP classrooms serving Title I students, those are not abstract questions. They explain specific patterns I had been watching for years without language to name.

My CI 702 presentation, a critical examination of Ralph Tyler and E.D. Hirsch through the lens of culturally responsive and student-centered pedagogy, documents where this theoretical synthesis began. Rather than accepting Tyler's structured rationale or Hirsch's core knowledge model at face value, I interrogated both for what they failed to address: metacognitive development, cultural responsiveness, and student agency. The argument I made in that presentation, that rigidity in curriculum design marginalizes students from non-dominant cultures and that content knowledge without thinking skills is insufficient preparation for real-world challenges, became foundational to the conceptual framework I built two semesters later. The progression from that critique to the framework is not coincidental. It reflects a consistent line of inquiry developing across the program.

Cognitive and Learning Theory

Vygotsky (1978) explained how higher-order reasoning develops through guided participation, not independent discovery. Flavell's (1979) distinction between metacognitive knowledge and metacognitive regulation gave me a framework for what I was observing in classrooms: students often knew they were struggling without knowing what to do about it. Schon's (1983) reflection-in-action put the responsibility back on teachers. When teachers make their own reasoning visible in real time, students gain access to a model of what thinking through complexity actually looks like.

"Teacher perception functions as the mediating variable. What teachers believe about their students' capacity shapes what they design. What they design shapes what students have the opportunity to practice. What students practice shapes what they develop."

Literature Engagement and Field Knowledge

Three annotated bibliographies completed in C-701 with Dr. Sochor document sustained engagement with the research base on AP equity, dual enrollment, gifted education, and the specific experiences of first-generation students and Black students in advanced academic settings. Kyburg, Hertberg-Davis, and Callahan's (2007) work on AP programs and talented minorities and Saffore's (2023) research on Black girls' postsecondary outcomes both show that access without intentional instructional support produces uneven results. Dixson's (2020) argument for reframing gifted education toward maximizing learning for all students connected directly to my developing framework around metacognition as infrastructure rather than enrichment.

Conceptual Framework

My Conceptual Framework, developed in EDF 725 with Dr. Yuan, synthesized these theoretical strands into a working model. Teacher perception mediates instructional design. Instructional design shapes metacognitive opportunity. Metacognitive opportunity influences student agency. Equity and rigor are not competing priorities; they become mutually reinforcing when instruction is explicitly designed to build students' capacity to regulate their own learning.

Rubric Element 03

Reflection

The portfolio rubric defines outstanding reflection as in-depth synthesis that demonstrates a clear connection between doctoral experiences and professional growth and development. That connection has been the most personal dimension of this program, and the most difficult.

Positionality and Identity

My Positionality Reflection, written for CI 704 with Dr. Yuan, was the first assignment that required me to examine the roots of my commitments rather than simply act on them. I am the first college graduate on both sides of my extended family. I grew up without access to advanced coursework in a conservative, working-class household. Those experiences created the urgency I brought into this work and they also created blind spots. Writing that reflection forced me to name the ways my savior complex, my assumptions about what underserved students needed, and my impatience with slow systemic change were all getting in the way of genuine equity work. Advocacy rooted in unexamined identity is still advocacy for yourself.

Reflexivity in Research

Early drafts of my pilot study interview protocol revealed something uncomfortable: I had written questions that assumed my conclusion. The language I chose, the way I framed the problem, the order of the prompts all pointed the participant toward the answer I expected. Revising those questions taught me that reflexivity is not a section of a research paper. It is something you have to practice at every stage of inquiry, including the stage where you think you already know what you are going to find.

"Advocacy rooted in unexamined identity is still advocacy for yourself. The program taught me to slow down long enough to figure out whose interests I was actually serving."

Reflection in Practice

The action research cycle produced a different kind of reflection. Partway through the three weeks, the AP Government teacher I was working with described starting to hear herself asking what rather than how during class. She said she caught it in the moment and changed the question. That is Schon's (1983) reflection-in-action happening in a real classroom. Watching it happen confirmed that the most meaningful learning often occurs while you are still inside the experience, not after you have processed it safely from a distance.

Rubric Element 06

Research

Two original research studies conducted outside of any single course requirement. Both are grounded in a real problem of practice. Both contribute to a clear dissertation trajectory.

Pilot Study: Teacher Perception and Metacognition

Conducted in EDF 625, the pilot study used a phenomenological design with semi-structured interviews and classroom observations across Title I and higher-SES AP settings. Thematic coding following Saldana (2011) revealed that teachers in higher-SES settings more frequently described students as independent, capable of abstraction, and self-directed. Teachers in Title I contexts more often used language like "building foundations," "meeting them where they are," and "being realistic about what they can handle." These phrases sound supportive. In practice, they corresponded with reduced opportunities for students to engage in independent metacognitive work. The problem was not student capacity. It was instructional expectation.

Action Research: Dunbar High School, FWISD

Research question: How does coaching an AP U.S. Government teacher to use AP Classroom collaboratively affect student engagement and metacognitive thinking over three weeks? Data collection included pre and post AP Classroom progress checks, weekly observation notes, student metacognitive exit tickets, weekly teacher reflections, and a researcher coaching log.

Key Findings

Individual student gains ranged from 32 to 50 percentage points. Every student completed the post-assessment, a shift from baseline when several left questions unanswered. Exit ticket responses evolved from brief and unspecific to strategy-based, with students identifying what helped them, explaining why, and naming what they would do differently next time. The teacher documented increasing intentionality in her questioning, moving from confirming correct answers to asking students to justify their reasoning.

"The most significant instructional shift was scaffolding used to support engagement with purpose, not to reduce cognitive demand, but to build students' capacity to manage it independently."

The pilot study produced qualitative findings on teacher perception and metacognitive practice across SES contexts. The action research documented measurable student growth through a theoretically grounded intervention. Both are organized, thorough, and directly inform the dissertation.
