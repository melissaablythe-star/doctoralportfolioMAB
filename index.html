Introduction

Where I Began & Where I Am

Advanced academic programming often appears equitable on paper. Enrollment increases, course offerings expand, and districts celebrate rising participation rates. Yet access alone does not ensure intellectual equity. In my work supporting Advanced Placement instruction across Fort Worth ISD, I repeatedly observed differences in how rigor was enacted across campuses. In one Title I classroom, students were completing structured note guides and preparing for multiple-choice practice, while in a higher-SES campus, students in the same course were engaged in peer critique, metacognitive reflection journals, and iterative revision of analytical writing.

Both classrooms were on pace. Both were aligned to standards. The cognitive work required of students, however, differed substantially.

"I entered this program focused on solving problems. I leave it focused on studying them, which turns out to be a more powerful way to produce lasting change."

This portfolio documents the development of my identity as a scholar-practitioner. The sections that follow address each element of the Marshall University Doctoral Portfolio Rubric: Collaboration, Depth of Understanding, Reflection, Scholarship, Communication, and Research.

Faculty Collaboration

Feedback from faculty extended beyond surface revision to substantive interrogation of theoretical alignment. When I brought early drafts of my conceptual framework for review, the question came back: where is the mechanism? That question forced a level of precision I had not previously applied. I had listed theories I valued. What I had not done was articulate how one thing actually produces another.

Peer Collaboration

A colleague challenged my framing of equity gaps, noting that describing students as lacking metacognitive skills implied the problem resided in the student rather than in the instructional opportunity. That exchange reshaped the language I use in research and the lens I apply in classrooms. I now consistently distinguish between what students have not yet been taught and what they are not capable of.

Practitioner Partnership

Working alongside an AP Government teacher at Dunbar High School, I engaged in co-planning, modeling, and collaborative analysis rather than prescriptive support. Midway through the action research cycle, the teacher reflected that the process had changed how she listened to herself during class. She described starting to hear herself asking what rather than how and deciding to shift the question.

Collaboration is evident across multiple contexts: faculty mentorship and peer dialogue within the program, co-presenting at the NSSA and SITE national conferences, and a sustained practitioner research partnership at Dunbar High School. Each experience strengthened the rigor of my work and deepened my understanding of what shared inquiry requires.

Rubric Element 02

Depth of Understanding

My understanding of teaching, learning, and curriculum deepened through sustained engagement with theoretical frameworks across the program. The most significant shift was conceptual: I moved from viewing instructional improvement as primarily structural to understanding it as philosophical.

Curriculum Theory

Eisner's (2002) framing of curriculum as an intellectual and aesthetic encounter disrupted my prior view of curriculum as content to be covered. Freire's (1970) pedagogy of critical consciousness raised questions about whose knowledge is centered and how power operates within advanced academic spaces.

Cognitive & Learning Theory

Vygotsky's (1978) concept of scaffolded social interaction explained how higher-order reasoning develops through guided participation rather than spontaneous mastery. Flavell's (1979) distinction between metacognitive knowledge and metacognitive regulation gave me a framework for analyzing classroom observations. Schon's (1983) reflection-in-action brought the teacher's role into focus.

"When teachers make their own thinking visible in real time, modeling how they approach complexity, revise, and reason, they create the conditions for students to develop those same capacities."

Conceptual Framework

My Conceptual Framework, developed in EDF 725, formalized the synthesis of these theoretical strands. Rather than treating equity, rigor, and metacognition as separate initiatives, I articulated them as interdependent constructs. Teacher perception functions as the mediating variable. What teachers believe about their students' capacity shapes what they design. What they design shapes what students have the opportunity to practice. What students practice shapes what they develop.

Section Summary — Depth of Understanding

My depth of understanding is demonstrated through sustained, synthesized engagement with curriculum theory, learning theory, and the emerging intersection of AI and cognition. The conceptual framework integrates Eisner, Freire, Vygotsky, Flavell, Schon, Hammond, and Zimmerman into a coherent model grounded in the specific inequities of AP instruction in underserved settings.

Rubric Element 03

Reflection

The portfolio rubric asks for deep reflection that demonstrates a clear connection between doctoral program experiences and professional growth. That connection has been the most personal and at times the most uncomfortable dimension of this program.

Positionality

My Positionality Reflection in EDF 725 was the first assignment that required me to examine the roots of my beliefs rather than simply act on them. Writing about my background as the first college graduate in my family, navigating professional spaces where I was frequently underestimated, and carrying a fierce urgency about outcomes for underserved students: all of that shaped how I led long before I could articulate it. The reflection revealed that this urgency, while rooted in genuine commitment, sometimes narrowed my patience for the pace of inquiry.

Research as Reflection

Learning to listen to interview data without imposing my framework before the participant spoke was harder than I expected. Early drafts of my pilot study interview protocol revealed that I had written questions that assumed my conclusion. Revising those questions taught me that reflexivity is not a section of a research paper. It is a discipline that has to be practiced throughout the entire inquiry process.

"The most meaningful learning happens when we examine our practice while we are still inside it." — Schon (1983)

Learning in Action

The action research cycle produced a different kind of reflection. Working in a classroom rather than analyzing one from the outside required me to hold my theoretical commitments lightly enough to respond to what was actually happening. Watching the teacher shift — not because I told her to, but because she observed her students doing things she had not thought they could do — was one of the clearest moments of this entire program.

Section Summary — Reflection

Reflection in this portfolio is not retrospective decoration. It is woven throughout the research process, from positionality work that surfaced hidden assumptions, to protocol revision that corrected leading questions, to real-time reflection during the action research cycle. The program produced a measurable shift in how I understand my role: from a leader who solves problems to a scholar-practitioner who studies them.

Rubric Element 05 & 06

Research

My research across this program demonstrates the utilization of the research process through a variety of projects that is highly organized and thorough, with research incorporated outside of the classroom.

Pilot Study: Teacher Perception & Metacognition

Conducted in EDF 625 using a phenomenological design with semi-structured interviews and classroom observations across Title I and higher-SES AP settings. Thematic coding following Saldana (2011) revealed that teachers in higher-SES settings more frequently described students as independent and capable of abstraction. Teachers in Title I contexts more often used language like "building foundations" and "being realistic about what they can handle."

The significance was not that scaffolding is harmful. The problem was that scaffolding frequently replaced metacognitive opportunity rather than building toward it.

Action Research: Dunbar High School, FWISD

Research question: How does coaching an AP U.S. Government teacher to use AP Classroom collaboratively affect student engagement and metacognitive thinking over three weeks?

Data collection included pre/post AP Classroom progress checks, weekly observation notes using a structured checklist, student metacognitive exit tickets, weekly teacher reflections, and a researcher coaching log.

Key Findings

Individual student gains ranged from 32 to 50 percentage points. Every student completed the post-assessment, a shift from baseline when several left questions unanswered. Exit ticket responses evolved from brief, unspecific answers to strategy-based explanations identifying what helped, why, and what they would change next time. The teacher documented increasing intentionality in her questioning, moving from confirming correct answers to asking students to justify their reasoning.

"The most significant instructional shift was the use of scaffolding to support engagement with purpose. It was not to reduce cognitive demand, but to build students' capacity to manage it independently."

Section Summary — Research

Two original research studies conducted outside of any single course requirement. The pilot study produced qualitative findings on teacher perception and metacognitive practice across SES contexts. The action research documented measurable student growth through a theoretically grounded intervention. Both are organized, thorough, and directly inform the dissertation ahead.
